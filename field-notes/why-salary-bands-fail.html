Why Salary Bands Fail — And What to Do Instead

April 18, 2026

Most organizations build salary bands the wrong way. They pull market data, pick a percentile, and call it a philosophy. The result: bands that look defensible on paper but collapse the moment a manager sits across the table from a candidate.

The problem isn't the data. It's the order of operations.

The Backwards Build

The typical process goes: collect survey data → define a target percentile (usually P50 because it sounds reasonable) → set band midpoints → declare victory. HR publishes a spreadsheet. Managers ignore it.

Why do they ignore it? Because the bands weren't built around how work is actually leveled in the organization. A band for "Senior Analyst" that spans $72k–$96k is useless if nobody can explain what makes someone a Senior versus an Analyst, or why the spread is 33% instead of 20% or 50%.

Bands without architecture are just ranges with a logo on them.

What a Working Band System Needs

Three things have to exist before you touch market data:

  1. A defined job architecture. Career levels, job families, and a clear distinction between what moves someone up a level versus across a family. Without this, you're putting precise numbers on imprecise categories.

  2. A pay philosophy that takes a position. "We target P50" is not a philosophy — it's a default. A real philosophy answers: P50 of what market? For which roles? How do we handle roles where the market is thin or irrelevant?

  3. A range spread rationale. Wider spreads mean more room for tenure and performance differentiation within a level. Narrower spreads push people up the ladder faster. Neither is wrong — but it has to be a deliberate choice, not an accident of how you imported the survey export.

The Build Sequence That Works

Start with your architecture. Define your levels, your families, your criteria for movement. Then layer in philosophy: where do you want to position against the market, and why? Only then bring in the data.

When you build in this order, the bands emerge from the structure rather than defining it. Managers understand the bands because they understand the levels. Candidates accept offers because the rationale is clear. Pay equity gaps are visible and addressable rather than buried in a spreadsheet nobody reads.

The Honest Caveat

This takes longer. A proper job architecture engagement — done right — is a 6-to-12 week project before you ever model a salary band. Organizations that want bands next month will be disappointed.

But organizations that want bands that still work in three years? That's the sequence.
